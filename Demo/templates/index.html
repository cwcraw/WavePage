A Particle in a Webpage

About This Project

This widget was created as a way to learn Python, particularly numpy, matplotlib and Django. It is an exploration of the classic 'particle in a box' problem which students encounter in their first semester of quantum mechanics or physical chemistry. You will be able to find plots of the first six energy levels of the time independent Schrödinger equation here.

Physics Background

A 'particle in a box' is a toy system used to introduce students to the basics of Quantum Mechanics and the Schrödinger equation. The Schrödinger Equation describes the behavior of a quantum system, such as an electron, in space in time. A key component of the equation is the potential. The potential describes the background environment the electron may find itself in. If it is near another electron or a nucleus, the potential would reflect this.

The underlying assumption of a 'particle in a box' is that the particle is equally likely to be in any position in side the box, but cannot leave the box. The Schrödinger Equation can reflect this by setting the potential to zero inside the box and infinity outside the box. You can then solve the Schrödinger Equation to get a description of the particles behavior in space and time - also known as the wave function. The square of the wave function is proportinal to the probibility of measuring a particles location at a certain point at a given time. by plotting the square of the wavefunction you can determine where you are likely to measure the location of the particle.

You can make the 'particle in a box' system more interesting by changing varying the potential inside the 'box.' Think of this a box with a sloped or bowl shaped bottom. In the examples created on this site we have 5 additional potentials. Three are interesting conceptually to help students understand what a wave function is and to demonstrate quantum tunnelling. These whould be the step potential and the two barrier potentials. The other two examples, the harmonic oscillator and the Morse potential are interesting from a chemist's point of view. They represent first steps to modelling an electron in an atom. Please visit the demonstrations for more details

There is one final wrinkle to keep in mind. Particles can have lower or higher energy. This will change where a particle is likely to be found - that is, the shape of the wave function. In our examples the energy exists at discrete values only and the first six values are shown.

For more information I would recommend reviewing the wikipedia pages on the Schrödinger equation or a particle in a box to cover the basics and give a more detailed explanation of the math involved.
